Peace of Mind

UX/UI Design, Prototyping | Aug ’16 - Dec ’16 | Individual Project

Peace of Mind is a 3-piece caregiving system for those taking care of the elderly. It not only reminds the patient when to take their medication(s), but also gives the caregiver a peace of mind when not near their loved ones.

Role: UX & UI Designer
Methods: Scenarios, Persona, Wireframe, User Testing, Interactive Prototyping, 3D Modeling
Tools: Balsamiq, Sketch, Invision, FramerJS, Fusion 360

OBJECTIVE

The main goal of this design was to give caretakers a peace of mind when they are away from their elderly loved ones by serving as a pill reminder and a checking method to ensure the overall well-being and status of the patient. Caregivers need to make sure that elderly patients take the the correct medication in a timely manner. Designing a system that solved for these user needs proved to be complicated as it had multiple pieces that had to be integrated with one another. The first is a mobile app for the caregiver so they can manage the system and receive notifications about the patient. The second is a pendant for the patient so they are reminded to take their pills. The third is the actual pill dispenser device that has an interface for the patient to use.

This was an individual project, so I had the opportunity to design all three components from low fidelity wireframes to high fidelity prototypes, and even to 3D print the pendant I designed.

INITIAL CONCEPTS

Scenario-Based Design

I started off this design by thinking of the average use case for using a system like this. Because I am also a caregiver for my grandmother, I had to make sure not to think of myself as a typical user. I talked to friends and family members who were caregivers for their loved ones. By doing so, I was able to create scenarios and personas that encompassed a range of users. The biggest concerns that people seemed to have when taking care of the elderly are:

  1. Making sure accurate and all medication is taken timely
  2. Making sure the patient has not fallen and cannot get up
  3. Check to see the patient's whereabouts

Flow Studies & Functionalities

I wanted to figure out the initial interaction between each player in the system. Once I drew out a simple flow, it became clear to see how they integrated with one another. The flow also highlighted the core functionalities in the system.

MOBILE APP DESIGN

Low Fidelity Prototype

I used Balsamiq to create the initial low fidelity prototype:

Medium Fidelity Prototype

After creating the low fidelity prototype and receiving feedback, I had the chance to learn how to use Sketch and Invision to create the medium fidelity prototype. Because I was more focused on functionality rather than the UI at this point, I did not want to focus on color decisions. By putting the Sketch screens into InVision and making it more interactive, I was able to user test with two caregivers.

Initial Feedback

For the mobile app, users felt that there were too many functions, especially the camera option to view the patient. They also felt that in an emergency, calling 911 first before using the app to send the information to the hospital would be more preferable. In addition, they wondered where they would be able to see missed pill information. Based on these feedbacks, I made adjustments to the prototype.

High Fidelity Prototype

For the high fidelity prototype, I designed the app based on the feedback received. I concentrated on the UI. In addition, I made the prototype more interactive in FramerJS.

PENDANT DESIGN

The pendant is a wearable necklace that serves as a pill reminder device, a GPS, a fall detection tracker, and a clock. It is worn around the patient’s neck and will display simple messages for them to read. There is no interaction except the main button so that patients would not feel overwhelmed by the device.

Pendant Mockup

For the pendant design, I went through several sketches before coming to my final design concept. Originally, I wanted to use a smart watch concept. However, I felt that this may hinder elderly patients arm movements if it was too heavy as it required an interface. In the end, I decided upon a pocket watch-inspired pendant design.

3D Model

The 3D model was mocked up in Fusion 360 and 3D printed. The pendant serves five different functionalities: telling the time, pill reminder, notifying of missed pill, fall detection tracker, and a GPS.

Reflection

  • "You are not your user!" As designers, this statement is drilled into our brains. For this project, I had to make sure that I was not designing for myself so I constantly talked to other caregivers to get their insights and feedbacks. This helped me develop empathy for my users and design a solution that addressed a true user need.
  • Going through all three levels of prototyping can become tedious but is important as you are going through design iterations. Because I had focused solely on the functionality for the medium fidelity prototype for the app, I was able to change certain parts without too much trouble once I got feedback.
