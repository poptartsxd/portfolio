AR Storybook

UX Design, AR Development, Interactive Storytelling | Aug ’16 - Dec ’16 | Partner Project

The AR Storybook is an interactive narrative that tells the history of Augmented Reality in an AR application and is built upon the ArgonJS framework.

My Role: UX Designer & Developer
Methods: User flows, Wireframe, Development, 3D Modeling
Tools: Sketch, Vuforia, Twine, ArgonJS, Github, Blender

OVERVIEW

Our goal was to tell the history of Augmented Reality in an AR medium. Argon is a Javascript framework designed to help develop applications that want to utilize AR technology. We built the storybook within the Argon browser by using the framework as well as various other AR tools, such as Vuforia and Twine. The 3D models were created with Blender. This project gave me my first experience working with augmented reality.

I focused on integrating Vuforia with Argon, constructing the images to trigger the AR components, and designing the interaction.

DESIGN

Narrative Concept

The history of AR goes back more than a century. Our narrative originally started out as a linear progression as it represented a timeline. We pinpointed 10 major events as a basis for our storybook and each would have its respective story:

  • The Sword of Damocles
  • Videoplace
  • Eyetap
  • Virtual Fixtures
  • ARToolkit
  • ARQuake
  • Google Glass Beta Test
  • Hololens
  • PokemonGO
  • ArgonJS Framework
Decision Design #1: Non-linear and interactive flow

The timeline may not be the most ideal as users may want to flip to different stories, prompting us to make the application non-linear.

Design Decision #2: Tap to read through story

Originally, we thought about using a scroll textbox, but because the user would have to keep holding the phone at the trigger image to show the text, scrolling may be difficult. We decided to have the user tap through different parts of the story.

Decision Decision #3: AR triggers override current story displayed

We had to figure out how the triggers would dictate the storyline. For example, if the app had Story 1 on the screen but the user switched the camera view to trigger #2, the device should now display Story 2.

Decision Decision #4: Keep application simple

As the application would be using a camera view with overlaid text and images, we wanted it to focus on the AR component. As a result, all the screens have the same format, with the AR component appearing at the top and the text at the bottom.

DEVELOPMENT

Image Recognition

Image recognition was done through the Vuforia database. The biggest challenge was creating images that had a "high target rating". The higher the target rating, the more stable the AR application as it could recognize the images better.

After 3 iterations, we were able to bump up all our images to at least 2/3 star ratings. At first, a lot of them were at 0 stars so some images could not be recognized at all. I learned that by adding asymmetrical components to the drawings, the target rating would increase.

3D Modeling

We made all 10 models in Blender. We were worried that the more complex the 3D model, the more processing power it would take to load the images. Instead, we created simple 3D models, some with texture and some with just one color.





3D models done by teammate

Interactive Narrative with Twine

Originally, we programmed the Vuforia and Argon capability by itself just to make sure that the AR components were triggering correctly. After ensuring all images were rendering correctly, we then decided to use Twine to make it more interactive.

FINAL PRODUCT

Storybook

All of these pieces worked cohesively to tell the story of AR in our storybook. Users would simply have to open the Argon app and go to our link. Then they could use the images (or just pull it up on their computer) to trigger the stories.

Reflection
  • Augmented Reality development is not as daunting as it seems. I took on this project because I wanted to learn how to implement an AR application, even though I did feel it was a little over my head. There was a lot to learn at first but once I understood the flow of information and how each piece worked with one another, it became easier to develop.
  • It's important to draw out story flows before jumping straight into implementation. Especially with an interactive story, we had to figure out user flows from different scenarios. It was very tempting to jump straight into coding. However, once we had drawn out the flows, programming the application made a lot more sense.
