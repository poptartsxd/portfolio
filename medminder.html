MedMinder

UX/UI Design, Prototyping | Aug ’16 - Dec ’16 | Individual Project



MedMinder is a 3-piece caregiving system for those taking care of the elderly. It not only reminds the patient when to take their medication(s), but also gives the caregiver a peace of mind when not near their loved ones.

My Role: UX & UI Designer
Methods: Scenarios, Persona, Wireframe, User Testing, Interactive Prototyping, 3D Modeling
Tools: Balsamiq, Sketch, Invision, FramerJS, Fusion 360

OVERVIEW

The main goal of this design was to give caretakers a peace of mind when they are away from their elderly loved ones by serving as a pill reminder and a checking method to ensure the overall well-being and status of the patient. Caregivers need to make sure that elderly patients take the the correct medication in a timely manner.

  • Mobile app: caregiver can manage system, receive notifications, and communicate with doctors
  • Pendant: patient can receive reminders to take their pills
  • Pill dispense device: patient can know which pill to take


PROCESS


INITIAL CONCEPTS

Scenario-Based Design

My target users are caregivers and patients. Caregiving for the elderly can add stress to a person's life. Talking to caregivers, I found the biggest concerns to be:

  1. Making sure accurate and all medication is taken timely
  2. Receiving quick answers from doctors about minor symptoms

MOBILE APP DESIGN

Functionality

Based on the scenarios and talking to users, the mobile app should:

  • Notify caregiver of missed pills
  • Allow caregiver to manage pills
  • Allow caregiver to communicate with doctors and nurses
Low Fidelity Prototype

I originally used Balsamiq to create the low fidelity prototypes. While it was an easy tool to learn, I felt that it was limiting as it can bias you towards creating an iOS app. I felt that sketching the wireframes gave you more freedom.

Workflow
Design Decision #1: List of medication

I added a timeline for the main medication page so that users can see what is the next upcoming pill as well as past pills taken.

Design Decision #2: Color coding pill status

Color coding the different statuses of pills allows the user to get historical information about accuracy and timeliness of medication.

In addition, for missed pills, users can send a reminder to the patient.

Design Decision #3: List of appointments

Because patients have different doctors for different symptoms, I added this screen to allow caregivers to better organize information.

This is separated into confirmed appointments and requested appointments that doctors have yet to process.

Design Decision #4: Requesting appointment

Caregivers can also send requests to doctors for an appointment. They can select the reason and an available date. This request will then be added to the "Requested" section.

Design Decision #5: Messaging doctors

I added the functionality of messaging doctors for quick questions that may not warrant a doctor visit. This allows fast and easy communication between doctor and caregiver.

PENDANT DESIGN

Sketches

For the pendant design, I went through several sketches before coming to my final design concept. Originally, I wanted to use a smart watch concept. However, I felt that this may hinder elderly patients arm movements if it was too heavy as it required an interface. In the end, I decided upon a pocket watch-inspired pendant design.

Design Decision #1: Pendant Design

The pendant is a wearable necklace that serves as a pill reminder device and a clock. I designed it to be worn around the patient's neck so it won't hinder any movement and will display simple messages for them to read.

Overall feedback for this design is that it is unique and simple for the patient to use.

Design Decision #2: Clock Feature

The two hands of the pendant will give the time. The black hand is the minutes hand and the silver is the hours hand.

I chose to physically represent the hands so that it is easy for the patient to tell what time it is.

Design Decision #3: Upcoming Pill Reminder

The pendant will vibrate twice 5 minutes before the next pill time. Or, the patient can press the button to show the next upcoming pill. When the button is pressed and held, the hands of the clock will change to the time for the next upcoming pill. When released, the hands will go back to current time.

Utilizing the clock allows the patient to easily see when they need to take their next pill as well as tell them what pill to take.



Design Decision #4: Missed Pill Reminder

The pendant will turn red if there is a missed pill. It will give a list of the pills that the user has missed and at what time they missed it.

The color of the missed pill notification is red so that the patient will be alerted. Having a vibration alert will also prompt them to look at the pendant.

Reflection

  • "You are not your user!" As designers, this statement is drilled into our brains. For this project, I had to make sure that I was not designing for myself so I constantly talked to other caregivers to get their insights and feedbacks. This helped me develop empathy for my users and design a solution that addressed a true user need.
  • Paper and pencil is the best way to go. I wanted to learn Balsamiq for the low fidelity prototype. However, I found that paper sketches was still the fastest and most fluid way to create low fidelity wireframes.
